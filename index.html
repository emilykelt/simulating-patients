Simulating More Accurate Patients: Cognitively-Biased Personas for Evaluating Medical LLMs

Emily Kelt
University of Cambridge
emilykelt/simulating-patients

Abstract

Bean et al. (2025) introduced HELPMed, a framework in which a GPT-4o-simulated patient consults a GPT-4o medical assistant across 10 NHS triage scenarios. Simulated patients achieved 52% disposition accuracy versus ~43% for real human participants, with a striking bimodal distribution: 26/30 scenario–condition pairs scored either 0% or 100%, unlike the graded 8–87% spread observed in humans.

We investigate whether injecting cognitive bias personas into the simulated patient system prompt can reduce this bimodal clustering and better match the human per-scenario accuracy profile. Five personas (Anchorer, Anxious Catastrophiser, Dismisser, Premature Closer, and Confidence Deferrer) were each evaluated at n=25 runs per scenario on GPT-4o. An optimal weighted mixture was derived by minimising mean squared error against the human per-scenario baseline using constrained optimisation (SLSQP).

The mixture (Anchorer 41.2%, Catastrophiser 33.4%, Dismisser 25.4%) reduced per-scenario RMSE from 38.8pp to 16.1pp in-sample (58%). Leave-one-scenario-out cross-validation gives a held-out RMSE of 23.1pp, a 41% generalisation reduction [95% CI: 15.4–29.2pp]. Experiments with Claude Haiku as patient model reveal that reduced RLHF alignment produces a standard accuracy of 46.4%, closer to the human 43.3%, and substantially stronger bias execution, with Catastrophiser falling to 24.8% versus 40% under GPT-4o.

Background

HELPMed and the Simulated Patient Problem

Bean et al. (2025) introduced HELPMed, a framework for evaluating LLM-based medical assistants using GPT-4o-simulated patients. Patients consult an assistant across 10 NHS triage scenarios and choose one of five disposition levels: Ambulance, A&E, Urgent Primary Care, Routine GP, or Self-care. Simulated patients achieved 52% overall accuracy against gold-standard NHS dispositions, compared to ~43% for real human participants.

Bean et al. explicitly noted a structural bimodality: 26/30 scenario–condition combinations produced either 0% or 100% accuracy. Human participants show a graded distribution ranging from 8% (Pneumonia) to 87% (Tinnitus). This gap in distributional validity limits the use of LLM-simulated patients as realistic proxies for human behaviour in medical education research.

RLHF Resistance and Persona Injection

Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) training makes models like GPT-4o helpful, harmless, and accurate, but also resistant to instructions that conflict with this training. Prior work (AgentClinic; Bias Runs Deep, Gupta et al. 2023) shows that stronger, more aligned models exhibit greater resistance to persona-induced bias, with GPT-4-Turbo showing the least persona-induced performance shift of models tested. This directly motivates testing whether less RLHF-constrained models (Claude Haiku) show stronger bias compliance.

Silicon Sampling Validity

The validity of using LLMs to simulate human populations ("silicon sampling") has been debated extensively (Gui & Toubia; Sarstedt et al.). A core concern is that LLMs' shared training distribution homogenises responses in ways that do not reflect real human heterogeneity. The bimodal pattern observed in HELPMed is a specific manifestation of this homogenisation problem.

Methods

Experimental Setup

We replicate the Bean et al. HELPMed framework exactly, using GPT-4o-2024-05-13 as both patient and assistant model (temperature 1.0 for patient, as in the original study). Experiments were also conducted using Claude Haiku (claude-haiku-4-5-20251001) as the patient model with GPT-4o as assistant. Each condition was run at n = 25 per scenario (250 total runs per condition). The primary metric is urgency-level disposition accuracy.

Cognitive Bias Personas

Five cognitive bias personas were designed as system prompt injections to the patient model. Each persona is grounded in established psychological and medical decision-making literature:

1
Anchorer
Fixates on the first symptom mentioned. Resists updating their assessment when new information contradicts the initial framing.
You are a patient calling a medical helpline. You are worried about a health problem. When the conversation started, one particular symptom was at the front of your mind and your thinking keeps returning to it — even if the assistant raises other possibilities, you find yourself anchoring back to that initial concern. You are not irrational, but your interpretation is shaped by that first impression. Respond naturally in short conversational turns. Do not mention that you are anchoring.
2
Anxious Catastrophiser
Over-escalates perceived severity. Interprets ambiguous symptoms as worst-case. Partially reassurable but worry tends to return.
You are a patient calling a medical helpline. You are prone to health anxiety and tend to catastrophise. When you notice a symptom, your mind jumps to serious or life-threatening causes. You may feel briefly reassured but your worry returns. You escalate how serious you describe your symptoms and push for more urgent interventions. Respond naturally in short conversational turns. Do not explicitly state that you are anxious.
3
Dismisser
Minimises symptoms and resists medical advice. Prefers to self-manage and is reluctant to seek help.
You are a patient calling a medical helpline. You tend to downplay your symptoms and are sceptical about whether you really need professional care. You would rather manage things yourself. When the assistant recommends seeking help, you push back — you're not sure it's that serious, you've had this before, you're probably fine. Respond naturally in short conversational turns. Do not explicitly state that you are dismissing your symptoms.
4
Premature Closer
Reaches a conclusion early and stops seeking further information, even when the situation warrants continued enquiry.
You are a patient calling a medical helpline. You have already formed a view about what is wrong with you before the conversation began. Once you've decided on an explanation, you stop looking for alternatives. You do not volunteer additional symptoms and are happy to accept the first reasonable explanation that fits your conclusion. Respond naturally in short conversational turns. Do not mention that you have reached a premature conclusion.
5
Confidence Deferrer
Excessively defers to the assistant's framing and recommendations, abandoning their own initial assessment.
You are a patient calling a medical helpline. You hold medical professionals in very high regard and tend to defer to their authority. Whatever the assistant suggests — even if it contradicts what you initially said — you tend to agree with and adopt their framing. If they suggest your symptoms are mild, you concede they are mild. If they suggest urgency, you immediately become more concerned. Respond naturally in short conversational turns. Do not explicitly state that you are deferring.

Optimal Mixture Derivation

An optimal weighted mixture of personas was derived analytically. The objective was to find weights wi ≥ 0, Σwi = 1 minimising the mean squared error between the predicted per-scenario accuracy and the human baseline:

\[\min_{\mathbf{w}} \sum_s \!\left(\sum_i w_i \cdot \mathrm{acc}_{i,s} - \mathrm{human}_s\right)^{\!2} \quad \text{s.t.} \quad w_i \geq 0,\; \textstyle\sum_i w_i = 1\]

Solved using scipy.optimize.minimize (SLSQP). Premature Closer and Confidence Deferrer converged to <0.5% weight and were excluded. Remaining weights were renormalised.

Bimodality Analysis

The standard published metric for bimodality is Sarle's coefficient \(b = (\hat{\gamma}_1^2 + 1)/\hat{\kappa}\) (Sarle 1990), but at n=10 scenarios it is underpowered and unstable. We therefore also report a simple descriptive count we call the extreme ratio: the proportion of scenarios whose accuracy falls below 20% or above 80%. This is not a published statistical test; it is an ad-hoc summary of how often a condition collapses to the 0%/100% endpoints. It is reported alongside the bimodality coefficient rather than in place of it.

Remaining Limitations

A formal linear-programming convex-hull test was run for each scenario: can the human per-scenario accuracy be expressed as a convex combination of persona accuracies? Two scenarios are provably outside this reachable space:

  • Renal Colic: Human 35.5%; all personas score ≥56%, making the human floor structurally unreachable regardless of mixture weights.
  • Subarachnoid Haemorrhage: Human 35.2%; minimum persona accuracy is 36.0%, just above the human value. LP infeasible.

Pneumonia (human 8.2%, persona range 4–64%) is formally in the convex hull; the per-fold prediction error of +19.5pp reflects weight instability under LOSO-CV rather than a hard structural limit. This distinction matters: Pneumonia's poor fit is correctable with more data or better-designed personas; Renal Colic and Subarachnoid Haemorrhage are fundamentally unreachable by prompt-level intervention.

Differential and Red-Flag Accuracy

Urgency accuracy is the primary metric replicating Bean et al., but the pipeline also scores differential diagnosis and red-flag identification. Standard GPT-4o achieves the highest differential accuracy (69.6%) while the Anchorer (despite highest urgency accuracy at 67.2%) has the lowest differential accuracy (54.4%), suggesting anchoring to the first symptom impairs condition identification even when urgency is correct.

Discussion

Why Most Biased Personas Outperform Standard

Counterintuitively, most bias personas increase overall accuracy relative to the standard model. The explanation: the standard GPT-4o patient over-escalates mild conditions (Common Cold, Gallstones near 0%) due to RLHF-driven medical caution. Most biases accidentally counteract this tendency: the Dismisser and Anchorer introduce under-escalation that corrects an existing baseline error rather than simulating human behaviour. Only the Catastrophiser compounds the over-escalation, producing the lowest accuracy (~40%) and the closest match to human overall performance, though for the wrong mechanistic reasons.

RLHF Resistance as a Structural Barrier

The Haiku experiments provide the clearest evidence for RLHF resistance as the primary mechanism preventing faithful bias execution. The Catastrophiser falls from 40% (GPT-4o) to 24.8% (Haiku), a 15pp drop consistent with GPT-4o's safety training actively suppressing the bias mid-conversation. The literature (Bias Runs Deep, Gupta et al. 2023; Role-Play Paradox, 2024) confirms this pattern: larger, more aligned models show less persona-induced performance shift.

Optimal Mixture as a Method

The SLSQP-derived mixture provides a principled, data-driven approach to calibrating simulated patients. Leave-one-scenario-out cross-validation confirms 41% RMSE generalisation (23.1pp held-out vs 38.8pp standard), substantially reducing but not eliminating the in-sample optimism of the 58% figure. The convergence of weights to three interpretable personas is consistent with clinical decision-making bias literature. Critically, the two scenarios outside the convex hull (Renal Colic, Subarachnoid Haemorrhage) represent hard limits of prompt-level intervention, addressable only through fine-tuning or scenario-specific augmentation.

Sim-to-Real Gap

The broader simulation-validity literature corroborates our findings. Araújo et al. (2026) demonstrate across 451 participants and 31 LLM simulators that simulators are systematically "excessively cooperative, stylistically uniform, and lack realistic frustration or ambiguity", precisely the characteristics we observe in standard GPT-4o patients. Their User-Sim Index (USI) provides a fidelity metric that could be applied to our persona conditions as future validation. Owoicho et al. (2025) similarly find that general model capability does not predict simulation fidelity, consistent with our observation that GPT-4o's RLHF alignment, rather than raw capability, is the primary driver of persona resistance.

Persona Consistency Audit

To rule out incoherent role-play as an explanation for the observed accuracy patterns, we applied Abdulhai et al.'s (NeurIPS 2025) three automated consistency metrics (prompt-to-line, line-to-line, and Q&A consistency) using GPT-4o (gpt-4o-2024-11-20, temperature=0) as a judge. We sampled 20 conversations per persona stratified by scenario (2 per condition × 10 conditions); each patient turn was scored 1–5 against the persona description, and each conversation was scored for cross-turn coherence and factual self-consistency.

Every persona scores well above the neutral 3.0 anchor on prompt-to-line alignment, and line-to-line and Q&A consistency are at or near ceiling across the board. This rules out a "the model is just being incoherent" explanation: patients reliably enact the assigned cognitive bias and do not contradict themselves about their own symptoms, history, or stance. The accuracy and bimodality patterns we report therefore reflect what the personas do at the decision level, not failed role-play. The Catastrophiser scores highest on prompt-to-line (4.95) yet drops most under Haiku (15pp): surface-level enactment is preserved while the bimodal escalation behaviour is selectively suppressed, consistent with RLHF acting at the decision boundary rather than the surface persona.

Future Work

Abdulhai et al.'s multi-turn RL fine-tuning reduces persona inconsistency by 55%, providing a training-based alternative to our prompt-injection approach for the two scenarios outside the convex hull. USI-based fidelity measurement (Araújo et al. 2026) and real-scenario deployment in a medical education platform (cf. MedSimAI, Khamees et al. 2025) are the natural next validation steps.

Conclusion

Cognitive bias personas substantially improve the marginal accuracy calibration of LLM-simulated patients in NHS triage without model fine-tuning. The bimodal response pattern identified by Bean et al. is not a structural property of GPT-4o; it is a consequence of the standard patient prompt. An optimal weighted mixture of three personas (Anchorer 41.2%, Catastrophiser 33.4%, Dismisser 25.4%) achieves a 41% RMSE reduction under leave-one-scenario-out cross-validation (23.1pp vs 38.8pp, [95% CI: 15.4–29.2pp]), with in-sample fit of 58% (16.1pp). A formal convex-hull LP test identifies two scenarios (Renal Colic, Subarachnoid Haemorrhage) as provably unreachable by any prompt-level mixture. Experiments with Claude Haiku confirm that RLHF alignment suppresses bias execution: the Catastrophiser falls below the human baseline only in the less-constrained model. A GPT-4o-as-judge consistency audit on Abdulhai et al.'s three metrics confirms personas are reliably enacted (prompt-to-line ≥ 4.55/5, line-to-line and Q&A near ceiling), so the remaining accuracy gap reflects decision-level RLHF resistance rather than failed role-play. USI-based fidelity measurement is the natural next step to quantify the remaining sim-to-real gap.
